9/8: Fimbriated Moray

Beluga whales are extremely sociable mammals that live, hunt and migrate together in pods, ranging from a few individuals to hundreds of whales. They are often called the canaries of the sea. Belugas are highly vocal and produce a series of chirps, clicks, whistles and squeals to convey important information to each other.

Along with bowhead whales and narwhals, beluga whales are one of the three whales that live only in Arctic waters. They are adapted to the icy cold waters of the Arctic and lack a fin on their back, a dorsal fin. Scientists think that beluga could have evolved without a dorsal fin for good reasons – it makes diving under sea ice easier and helps to reduce heat loss. Instead of a dorsal fin, belugas have a dorsal ridge that is used to break thin sea ice.

Other facts:

1. Beluga whales' dives may last up to 25 minutes and can reach depths of 800 meters.

2. The word beluga comes from the Russian word "bielo" meaning white. However, these white whales are born dark gray. It can take up to eight years before they turn completely white.

3. The beluga is able to swim backwards.

4. The beluga can change the shape of its bulbous forehead, called a "melon", by blowing air around its sinuses.

5. In 2009, a captive beluga whale rescued a distressed participant of a free diving competition by pushing her to the surface.

6. The vertebrae in a beluga’s neck is not fused together, giving it the unusual ability to turn its head up, down and side-to-side.

7. Threats to beluga whales include climate change, hunting, oil and gas development, and industrial and urban pollution. Polar bears and killer whales are known predators of belugas throughout their Arctic range.
